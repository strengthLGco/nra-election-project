Our Mission & History

The National Rifle Association of America (NRA) was founded in 1871 by Union veterans Col. William C. Church and Gen. George Wingate to promote and encourage rifle marksmanship among citizens. Today, it is the most influential gun rights lobbying organization in the United States.

The NRA's mission centers on protecting the Second Amendment of the U.S. Constitution, which guarantees "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms." As a political interest group, the NRA wields significant influence in American elections through candidate endorsements, campaign contributions via its Political Action Committee (NRA-PVF), grassroots voter mobilization, and legislative lobbying through the NRA Institute for Legislative Action (NRA-ILA).

With over 5 million dues-paying members, the NRA is one of the most powerful interest groups in American politics. It scores and grades politicians on their gun rights voting records and invests heavily in elections at the federal, state, and local levels.

Our Coalition

Our Base Voters

Understanding the Americans who stand with the NRA to defend their constitutional freedoms.

Demographics

NRA supporters are predominantly white males aged 35–65+, though membership spans all demographics. They are concentrated in rural and suburban communities across the South, Midwest, and Mountain West. Many are middle-income to upper-middle-income earners ($50K–$150K), with backgrounds in blue-collar trades, law enforcement, military service, and small business ownership.

Rural America Veterans Suburban Blue Collar

Motivations

NRA voters are driven by a deep conviction that the Second Amendment is a fundamental American right. Core motivations include: self-defense and personal protection, hunting and outdoor recreation traditions, opposition to gun control legislation they see as government overreach, and preservation of a cultural identity tied to responsible firearms ownership.

2nd Amendment Self-Defense Anti-Overreach

Political Profile

NRA base voters are overwhelmingly registered Republicans or conservative-leaning Independents. Gun rights consistently rank as a top-3 voting issue for these voters. They are single-issue voters in many cases—an "F" rating from the NRA can end a candidacy in rural districts. They are highly motivated, high-propensity voters with above-average turnout rates.

Republican Single-Issue High Turnout

Geographic Strongholds

NRA membership is strongest in states with deep hunting traditions and permissive firearms laws: Texas, Florida, Pennsylvania, Ohio, Georgia, North Carolina, Virginia, and across the Mountain West (Montana, Wyoming, Idaho). Rural counties in swing states are critical NRA territory where even modest turnout boosts can decide statewide races.

NRA-PVF

Political Victory Fund

The NRA's Political Action Committee (PAC) that directly contributes to pro-Second Amendment candidates' campaigns. The NRA-PVF evaluates candidates based on their voting records, public statements, and questionnaire responses. It then issues letter grades (A+ through F) and endorsements. In the 2020 election cycle, the NRA-PVF spent over $29 million on federal elections, making it one of the most active PACs in American politics.

NRA-ILA

Institute for Legislative Action

The NRA's lobbying arm, established in 1975, works to influence legislation at the federal, state, and local levels. The NRA-ILA employs full-time lobbyists in Washington D.C. and every state capital. It drafts model legislation (like "Stand Your Ground" and concealed carry reciprocity laws), tracks bills, mobilizes grassroots campaigns, and coordinates with allied organizations to protect gun rights through the legislative process.

Staying Connected

NRA members stay engaged through a network of publications, digital media, and events. Members receive one of three flagship magazines: American Rifleman, American Hunter, or America's 1st Freedom. They also receive email action alerts from the NRA-ILA, access to NRA TV and podcast content, invitations to the NRA Annual Meetings & Exhibits Show (drawing 80,000+ attendees), local chapter events, firearms training courses, and exclusive member competitions and shooting events.

Our Pick

Candidate Endorsement

Analyzing the ideal pro-Second Amendment candidate profile the NRA would back in a presidential general election.

Ideal NRA Candidate

NRA A+ Rated • Republican • Pro-2A Champion

Strengths

  • Consistent voting record defending Second Amendment rights across state and federal office, with concrete legislative accomplishments (e.g., signing concealed carry or constitutional carry bills)
  • Personal connection to firearms culture — a gun owner, hunter, or sport shooter who authentically represents NRA members' values, not just a political convenience
  • Strong electability in a general election: swing-state appeal, crossover appeal to rural Democrats and independent voters, strong debate skills, and charismatic campaign presence
  • Commitment to appointing constitutionalist federal judges and Supreme Court justices who will uphold Heller and Bruen precedents protecting individual gun rights
  • Opposition to federal gun control measures: assault weapons bans, universal background check expansions, red flag laws, and ATF overreach on pistol braces and stabilizers

Potential Risks

  • History of bipartisan compromise on gun legislation could alienate hardline NRA members who view any concession as a slippery slope
  • Polarizing statements on social issues unrelated to guns could limit broad coalition-building and give media ammunition to attack by association
  • Vulnerability to being framed as "extreme" on guns after mass shooting events, requiring careful media strategy and rapid-response messaging

NRA Endorsement Criteria

01

Legislative Record

Candidates are scored on every gun-related vote cast during their career. The NRA-PVF assigns letter grades from A+ to F. Only candidates with consistent A or A+ ratings earn the endorsement. A single vote for a gun control bill can drop a grade.

02

Questionnaire Responses

Candidates complete a detailed NRA-PVF questionnaire covering their positions on concealed carry, magazine limits, "assault weapon" bans, red flag laws, ATF funding, and Second Amendment philosophy. Answers are compared against voting records for consistency.

03

Electability Analysis

The NRA strategically endorses candidates who can win. An ideologically perfect candidate who can't win the general election doesn't advance the cause. The NRA evaluates polling, fundraising strength, campaign infrastructure, and electoral history.

04

Judicial Commitment

For presidential endorsements, the candidate's stated approach to judicial appointments is critical. The NRA needs assurance that federal judge nominees will uphold the individual rights interpretation of the Second Amendment established in Heller (2008) and expanded in Bruen (2022).

05

In-Office Advocacy

Beyond campaigning, the NRA needs a president who will actively champion gun rights once in office: vetoing gun control bills, using executive orders to protect gun owners, directing ATF to reduce regulatory burden, and using the bully pulpit to normalize responsible gun ownership.

Cultivating Friendly Media

The NRA maintains relationships with friendly outlets through a multi-pronged strategy: providing exclusive access to NRA leadership for interviews and op-eds, sponsoring segments on gun safety and shooting sports, offering press credentials at NRA events to friendly journalists, supplying rapid-response soundbites after breaking news events, and producing its own media content (NRA TV, podcasts, social media) to bypass hostile gatekeepers entirely.

When engaging hostile media, the NRA sends media-trained spokespeople who can pivot to core messaging — framing gun rights as civil rights, emphasizing law-abiding gun owners vs. criminals, and redirecting conversations from the weapon to the root causes of violence (mental health, enforcement failures, etc.).

The Game Plan

Three-Point Advocacy Plan

How the NRA gets its message through candidates, media, and the grassroots to win this election.

01

Arm Candidates with Messaging

Ensure every NRA-endorsed candidate is fluent in Second Amendment talking points and can defend gun rights on any debate stage or interview.

  • Provide candidates with NRA-tested messaging guides covering the most common gun control arguments and research-backed rebuttals
  • Host pre-debate briefing sessions and opposition research packets so endorsed candidates never get caught flat-footed on 2A issues
  • Deploy NRA surrogates (veterans, law enforcement, competitive shooters) to campaign with candidates and add credibility to their pro-gun stance
  • Stage high-visibility events: candidate speeches at NRA Annual Meeting, joint appearances at gun ranges, and community self-defense seminars
02

Dominate the Information Space

Control the narrative on gun rights through earned media, paid media, and owned media channels before opponents can define the terms.

  • Run targeted TV, radio, and digital ads in battleground states that frame the election as a choice between freedom and government overreach
  • Flood social media with shareable content: infographics on self-defense statistics, testimonials from women and minority gun owners, and "myth vs. fact" posts countering gun control narratives
  • Rapid-response team to push back in real-time on social media when anti-NRA narratives emerge — particularly after high-profile incidents
  • Earned media blitz: NRA spokespeople booked across all friendly outlets simultaneously during key news cycles
03

Mobilize the Membership Base

Convert 5 million members into a ground-level army of voter contacts, door-knockers, and Get Out The Vote (GOTV) organizers.

  • NRA-ILA action alerts: targeted emails and texts to members in battleground states with specific instructions — who to vote for, when, where, and why
  • Deploy volunteer phone banks and door-to-door canvassers in key precincts, focusing on low-propensity gun owners who may not vote without a push
  • Use NRA membership data to micro-target: identify unregistered gun owners, register them, and drive them to the polls with personalized outreach
  • Host "Freedom rallies" and gun shows in swing states during the final weeks — combining entertainment, voter registration drives, and candidate appearances

Political Influence

Attracting Candidate Support

Why candidates seek the NRA endorsement and what policies drive the relationship.

What Candidates Gain

The NRA Endorsement Seal

An NRA A-rating is political gold in Republican primaries and rural general election districts. It signals to millions of single-issue voters that this candidate will protect their rights. For many voters, the NRA grade is the only endorsement that matters.

Campaign Funding

NRA-PVF directly contributes to endorsed candidates and runs independent expenditure campaigns (TV ads, mailers, digital) on their behalf. In competitive races, NRA independent expenditures can exceed $10 million, providing a massive financial boost in the final stretch.

Voter Mobilization Machine

The NRA's 5 million members are a built-in volunteer army. NRA-ILA voter contact programs, phone banks, and door-knocking operations can deliver thousands of additional votes in tight races — the difference between winning and losing in swing states.

Media Amplification

NRA social channels, publications, and events give endorsed candidates direct access to millions of engaged, politically active Americans without going through mainstream media filters. An NRA Annual Meeting keynote speech reaches more gun owners than any cable news interview.
